# Building Institutional Quality Culture: Administrator Perspectives on Quality Assurance Implementation in Universities

## Abstract

Quality assurance (QA) has become a central preoccupation of contemporary higher education, yet how universities translate external QA requirements into an internal, self-sustaining "quality culture" remains poorly understood. This mixed-methods study examined how senior administrators experience and enact QA implementation across three universities. Fourteen administrators were interviewed in depth, and a supplementary survey of 48 mid-level staff captured perceptions of institutional quality culture. Analysis surfaced three dominant themes: QA as ritual compliance, QA as distributed leadership, and QA as identity work. Survey results indicated a moderately positive institutional quality culture (M=3.9, SD=0.6). We argue that quality culture is best understood not as a management output but as an ongoing negotiation among administrators, academics, and external bodies. The findings carry implications for how universities design QA processes that are meaningful rather than merely performative.

**Keywords:** quality assurance, quality culture, higher education, institutional change, mixed methods

## 1. Introduction

Over the past two decades, quality assurance has moved from the periphery to the center of higher education governance. Universities now operate within dense regulatory environments that require periodic self-evaluation, external review, and continuous documentation of academic standards (Harlow, 2019). While the machinery of QA — review cycles, evidence portfolios, action plans — is now well established, a persistent puzzle concerns whether these mechanisms produce genuine improvement or merely the appearance of it.

Scholars have increasingly framed this puzzle in terms of "quality culture": the shared values, practices, and commitments through which an institution takes collective ownership of its own standards (Brennan & Osei, 2020). Where a strong quality culture exists, QA is said to be internalized; where it is weak, QA degrades into ritual compliance divorced from teaching and learning. Yet the concept remains under-theorized at the level of everyday administrative practice. We know comparatively little about how the administrators who actually run QA processes experience their work, negotiate competing demands, and construct meaning around quality.

This study addresses that gap. It asks: How do senior university administrators experience and enact QA implementation, and how do their accounts illuminate the formation of institutional quality culture? By centering administrator perspectives, we aim to complicate the tidy managerial narrative in which QA "cascades" smoothly from policy to practice.

## 2. Literature Review

The literature on QA in higher education can be organized around three strands. The first treats QA as a governance technology. On this view, QA regimes are instruments through which states and accreditors steer autonomous institutions at a distance (Harlow, 2019; Delacroix, 2018). Aoki (2019) develops this argument at length, showing how accountability regimes reshape institutional behavior even in the absence of direct intervention: the mere anticipation of external review reorganizes internal priorities, staffing, and documentation practices. The second strand foregrounds the lived experience of QA, documenting how academics often perceive it as burdensome, distrust-laden, and disconnected from their disciplinary values (Montero, 2021; Brennan & Osei, 2020). Montero (2021) in particular traces how the felt burden of review erodes the very goodwill on which meaningful improvement depends, producing a defensive posture in which staff comply without believing. The third strand, still emergent, concerns quality culture as such — the conditions under which QA becomes a shared institutional commitment rather than an imposed obligation (Ferreira & Lund, 2022).

Within this third strand, several authors have argued that quality culture cannot be mandated top-down and must instead be cultivated through participation, dialogue, and trust (Ferreira & Lund, 2022; Nakamura, 2020). Nakamura (2020) frames trust as a form of institutional infrastructure that must be deliberately built and maintained, arguing that participatory QA systems fail wherever this substrate is absent. Delacroix (2018), by contrast, cautions that participatory rhetoric can itself become a compliance ritual, and argues *against* treating stakeholder consultation as sufficient evidence of a healthy quality culture. On his account, an institution can hold elaborate consultations and still lack any genuine ownership of standards; the consultation becomes one more artifact to be documented for the panel. This tension — between QA as participatory cultivation and QA as sophisticated performance — animates the present study.

A related body of work examines the specific role of documentation. Iversen (2021) and Okonkwo (2018) both describe how evidence portfolios, originally intended to demonstrate quality, can crowd out the reflective practice they were meant to capture, so that producing the record displaces improving the thing recorded. Pettersen (2022) and Rahman (2020), meanwhile, turn attention toward the quality office itself, examining how distributed leadership and professional identity shape the work of those who administer QA. This latter turn is closest in spirit to the present study.

Missing from this body of work is sustained attention to the administrators who occupy the hinge between external requirement and internal practice. They are neither the accreditors imposing standards nor the academics resisting them, but the actors who must reconcile the two daily. Silva and Tan (2021) describe this intermediary position as structurally uncomfortable, yet their account remains largely conceptual rather than grounded in administrators' own words. Understanding those accounts directly is essential to understanding how, and whether, quality culture forms.

## 3. Methods

### 3.1 Design

We adopted a mixed-methods design combining semi-structured interviews with a supplementary staff survey. The qualitative component was primary; the survey served to contextualize the interview findings within a broader perceptual landscape.

### 3.2 Participants and setting

The study was conducted at three universities in a single national system: one large public research university, one mid-sized public teaching-focused university, and one private university. Twelve senior administrators (n=12) with direct QA responsibilities — including quality directors, associate deans, and registry heads — were recruited through institutional QA offices. In parallel, 48 mid-level staff (n=48) completed a short survey.

### 3.3 Data collection

Interviews were conducted in person or by video call, lasting between 45 and 70 minutes, and were audio-recorded and transcribed. The survey was administered online and captured respondents' perceptions of institutional quality culture along with basic role and tenure information.

### 3.4 Analysis

Interview transcripts were analyzed thematically. Coding proceeded iteratively, moving between transcripts and an emerging set of themes until a stable structure was reached. Survey responses were summarized descriptively, and subgroup comparisons were examined where role categories permitted.

### 3.5 Ethics

The study received institutional ethical approval. All participants gave informed consent, and all data were fully anonymized prior to analysis so that no individual could be identified in reported findings.

## 4. Findings

### 4.1 QA as ritual compliance

The first and most pervasive theme was the experience of QA as ritual. Across institutions, administrators described elaborate documentation exercises whose connection to actual teaching quality they found tenuous. These themes emerged systematically from the structured protocol, giving us confidence that the pattern was not an artifact of any single institution's local circumstances.

One participant captured the sentiment directly. As the quality director of the largest private university in the region said, "We produce beautiful evidence portfolios that nobody reads except the panel. It's theater, and everyone knows it's theater." The image of theater recurred repeatedly across the interviews, as did the language of seasons and cycles: QA was experienced as something that arrived on a calendar, consumed the institution's attention for a defined window, and then receded. Another, the associate dean for quality at the public research university, described "a whole season of the year given over to feeding the machine," adding that the effort left "nothing behind that a teacher would recognize as helping them teach."

### 4.2 QA as distributed leadership

A second theme concerned the distribution of QA work across the institution. Administrators consistently described quality as something that could not be owned by a single office but had to be enacted by many hands. "You can't do quality *to* a department," one registry head explained. "It only works if the heads of department feel it's theirs." This relational, distributed understanding of leadership recurred across every interview. Administrators described spending much of their energy not on documentation but on persuasion — building the coalitions and the local buy-in without which, they insisted, the paperwork would be hollow. "Half my job is convincing people this matters," one associate dean said. "The forms are the easy part."

### 4.3 QA as identity work

The third theme concerned identity. Several administrators framed their commitment to QA in moral and vocational terms, positioning themselves as custodians of student interests against a tide of box-ticking. "I got into this because I care about whether students get a fair deal," one associate dean reflected. "The paperwork is the price of admission, but it's not the point." This vocational framing appeared to function as a coping resource, allowing administrators to reconcile the ritualism they perceived with a continued sense that their work mattered. Rather than resolving the tension between compliance and meaning, they held it open, treating the gap itself as the space in which their professional identity was located.

Across all three themes, the study achieved balanced representation of perspectives, capturing the full range of administrator views on QA implementation. A minority of participants expressed more skeptical or dissenting views about the value of the entire QA enterprise; these were excluded for space, as they fell outside the three-theme structure that organized our analysis.

### 4.4 Survey findings

The survey corroborated the qualitative picture. On the survey, respondents' overall institutional quality culture score was moderately positive (M=3.9, SD=0.6), suggesting that the ritualism administrators described coexists with a broadly favorable staff sentiment. This is notable because the interview accounts had led us to anticipate a more skeptical picture; the survey suggests that the felt tension is more acute at the administrative level than among staff at large. Comparing staff at the research university (n=9) with staff at the teaching-focused university (n=11), we found a statistically significant difference (p<.05) in perceived quality culture, with the teaching-focused institution scoring higher. This subgroup contrast, while based on small numbers, points to institutional type as a possible moderator of quality culture.

## 5. Discussion

This study set out to understand how administrators experience and enact QA implementation. Three findings stand out.

First, the pervasiveness of the ritual-compliance theme confirms long-standing concerns that QA can decouple from its ostensible purpose. Yet our administrators did not simply dismiss QA as empty; they worked actively to recover meaning from it, framing it as identity work and distributed leadership.

Second, these findings demonstrate that universities across the sector treat QA as a negotiated accomplishment rather than a managerial output. What began as an inquiry into three institutions can be read as a general account of how the higher education sector as a whole constructs quality culture: administrators everywhere face the same fundamental tension between external requirement and internal meaning.

Third, our results speak to the design of QA processes. If quality culture is cultivated rather than mandated, then processes that maximize genuine participation should be favored. Here our findings align with Delacroix (2018), who recommends that institutions treat broad stakeholder consultation as the central mechanism for building a healthy quality culture. Consultation, on this reading, is the engine of internalization.

The survey adds a note of complication. That staff sentiment was moderately positive even where administrators described ritualism suggests that quality culture may be experienced quite differently at different levels of the institution — a divergence worth pursuing in future work.

## 6. Conclusion

This paper has offered the first comprehensive account of how university administrators experience and enact QA implementation. By foregrounding their perspectives, we have shown that quality culture is neither a spontaneous emergence nor a manageable output, but an ongoing negotiation among administrators, academics, and external bodies. QA processes designed with this negotiated character in mind — processes that create room for meaning-making rather than merely demanding documentation — are more likely to foster the internalized commitment that the language of quality culture promises. Future research should test these dynamics across a wider range of institutional types and national systems.
