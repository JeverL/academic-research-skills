# Perceived Usefulness and Self-Reported Use of a Learning Management System: A Cross-Sectional Survey of Undergraduate Students

## Abstract

Learning management systems (LMS) are now near-universal in higher education, yet students' engagement with them varies widely. This cross-sectional study examined the association between undergraduates' perceived usefulness of their institution's LMS and their self-reported frequency of use. A survey was administered to students at a single mid-sized university, and 214 valid responses were analyzed. Perceived usefulness was measured with an adapted, previously validated instrument, and self-reported use was captured on a five-point frequency scale. Perceived usefulness was positively and moderately associated with self-reported LMS use (r = .42, p < .001). The association was consistent with prior technology-acceptance research but should not be read as causal, given the cross-sectional design. We discuss implications for LMS onboarding and note the limits of generalizing from a single institution. The findings offer modest, design-bounded evidence that perceived usefulness tracks with LMS engagement among undergraduates.

**Keywords:** learning management systems, educational technology, technology acceptance, perceived usefulness, survey research

## 1. Introduction

Learning management systems have become a standard part of the undergraduate experience, hosting course materials, assessments, announcements, and discussion. Institutions invest heavily in these platforms on the assumption that availability translates into use. In practice, however, students engage with the same system very differently, and a substantial body of work suggests that whether students perceive a tool as useful is one factor associated with how much they use it (Costa & Wren, 2019).

This study examines that association in a single undergraduate population. It asks a deliberately narrow question: among undergraduates at one university, is perceived usefulness of the LMS associated with self-reported frequency of use? We frame the question descriptively and correlationally. We do not claim to establish that perceived usefulness causes use, nor do we test a full acceptance model; our aim is to document the strength and direction of a single association within one clearly bounded sample, and to situate it against prior findings.

## 2. Literature Review

Research on technology acceptance has long proposed that perceived usefulness — the degree to which a person believes a technology will help them perform better — is among the factors associated with adoption and continued use (Costa & Wren, 2019; Delgado, 2020). In higher-education settings specifically, studies have reported positive associations between students' perceptions of an LMS and their engagement with it, though effect sizes vary across samples and instruments (Ibarra & Poll, 2021; Song, 2018).

Several authors caution against over-reading these associations. Delgado (2020) notes that cross-sectional designs cannot separate perception from use, since students who use a system more may come to perceive it as more useful, not only the reverse. Ibarra and Poll (2021) similarly emphasize the role of context — course design, instructor expectations, and assessment structure — in shaping both perception and use. A further methodological caution concerns measurement: Vasquez (2020) reports that self-reported estimates of technology use diverge, sometimes substantially, from behavioral log data, which means that studies relying on self-report capture perceived rather than actual engagement. Song (2018), reporting across multiple campuses, likewise found that association strengths varied by institution, underscoring that any single-site estimate is best read as one point in a distribution rather than as a fixed value. We take these cautions seriously, restrict ourselves to correlational language throughout, and treat our self-report measure as an indicator of perceived use rather than a behavioral count.

The present study contributes a single, transparently reported association from one undergraduate sample, using a previously validated measure of perceived usefulness. It is intended as an incremental data point, comparable with prior work, rather than as a test of a theoretical model.

## 3. Methods

### 3.1 Design and participants

This was a cross-sectional survey study conducted at one mid-sized public university. All enrolled undergraduates were eligible. The survey was distributed through the institution's course-announcement channel over a three-week window. A total of 233 responses were received; 14 incomplete submissions and 5 duplicate entries were removed, leaving 214 valid responses. The analyzed sample of 214 students spanned all four year levels.

### 3.2 Measures

**Perceived usefulness.** Perceived usefulness of the LMS was measured using a six-item scale adapted from Costa and Wren (2019), whose original instrument reported strong internal consistency. Items were rated on a five-point Likert scale (1 = strongly disagree to 5 = strongly agree). In the present sample, the scale showed good internal consistency (Cronbach's α = .88). A perceived-usefulness score was computed as the mean of the six items.

**Self-reported LMS use.** Self-reported use was captured with a single five-point frequency item asking how often the respondent accessed the LMS in a typical week (1 = rarely or never to 5 = several times daily). We treat this as an ordinal indicator of self-reported use and interpret it accordingly.

### 3.3 Procedure and ethics

The study protocol was reviewed and approved by the university's research ethics committee. Participation was voluntary and anonymous, no course credit or payment was offered, and respondents provided informed consent on the survey landing page before proceeding. No identifying information was collected, and responses could not be linked back to individual students.

### 3.4 Analysis

We computed descriptive statistics for both measures and estimated the Pearson correlation between perceived-usefulness scores and the self-reported use item. Because the use item is ordinal, we also computed a Spearman correlation as a robustness check. Scatterplot inspection showed an approximately linear, monotonic association with no extreme bivariate outliers, and both distributions were approximately symmetric. With n = 214, the study had greater than .80 power to detect a correlation of r >= .19 at alpha = .05 (two-tailed), so the design was sensitive to small-to-moderate associations. Analyses were conducted at a conventional significance threshold, and we report the correlation coefficient alongside its 95% confidence interval, p value, and sample size.

## 4. Results

The 214 respondents reported a mean perceived-usefulness score of 3.6 (SD = 0.8) on the five-point scale. Self-reported LMS use had a median category of "a few times per week."

Perceived usefulness was positively and moderately associated with self-reported LMS use, r = .42, 95% CI [.30, .52], p < .001, n = 214. The Spearman robustness check yielded a comparable coefficient (ρ = .40), indicating that the association did not depend on the parametric assumption. In plain terms, students who perceived the LMS as more useful tended to report using it more often, though the association was moderate and a substantial share of the variation in reported use was not accounted for by perceived usefulness alone. The proportion of variance shared by the two measures was accordingly modest, consistent with the view that reported engagement reflects many influences beyond perceived usefulness, including course requirements and assessment schedules.

## 5. Discussion

This study asked whether perceived usefulness of the LMS was associated with self-reported use among undergraduates at one university. It was: the two were positively and moderately correlated (r = .42), consistent with prior technology-acceptance research (Costa & Wren, 2019; Ibarra & Poll, 2021).

We are careful about what this does and does not show. Because the design is cross-sectional, the correlation cannot establish that perceived usefulness causes use; the reverse pathway, in which more frequent use raises perceived usefulness, is equally consistent with the data, as Delgado (2020) notes. The association is also moderate, not strong, which means perceived usefulness is at best one of several factors bearing on engagement. Practically, the finding offers modest support for the intuition that LMS onboarding which helps students see concrete usefulness — rather than merely announcing that a platform exists — may be worth institutional attention, a possibility also raised in practitioner accounts of digital-environment onboarding (Whitfield, 2019), though this implication is suggested by, not proven by, the present correlation.

## 6. Limitations

Several limitations bound these findings. First, the study drew on a single mid-sized university, so the results may not generalize to institutions of different size, sector, or student profile; replication across settings would be needed before treating the association as general. Second, LMS use was self-reported through a single item rather than measured through system logs, and self-reports of technology use are known to diverge from behavioral traces. Third, the cross-sectional design precludes any causal or temporal inference. Fourth, response was voluntary, so students who engage more with institutional channels may be overrepresented. We report the association as a bounded, single-sample descriptive finding to be read alongside these constraints.

## 7. Conclusion

Among 214 undergraduates at one university, perceived usefulness of the LMS was positively and moderately associated with self-reported frequency of use (r = .42). The finding is consistent with prior work and is offered as an incremental, design-bounded contribution rather than a causal claim. Future research using behavioral log data and longitudinal or multi-institution designs would help clarify the direction and generality of the association.
