# Dashboard Engagement and Course Retention: Evidence from an Undergraduate Learning Analytics Deployment

## Abstract

Learning analytics dashboards are widely promoted as tools that help students monitor their own progress, yet the empirical basis for their effect on retention remains thin. This study examines the relationship between student engagement with a learning analytics dashboard and end-of-term course retention in a large undergraduate course. Using LMS activity logs and a self-report survey collected from 142 students at Meridian State University, we measured dashboard engagement, self-regulated learning behavior, and course persistence. Dashboard engagement correlated positively with retention (r = .42), and students in the high-engagement group reported higher perceived control over their learning. We conclude that increasing dashboard engagement is a promising lever for improving retention in undergraduate courses. Implications for instructional design and institutional deployment are discussed.

**Keywords:** learning analytics, student dashboards, course retention, self-regulated learning, higher education

## 1 Introduction

Undergraduate attrition remains a persistent concern for institutions and instructors alike, and the first-year gateway course is frequently identified as a point of elevated risk. Over the past decade, learning analytics has emerged as a field promising to convert the digital traces students leave in learning management systems into actionable signals. Among the most visible products of this movement is the student-facing learning analytics dashboard, a visual interface that reflects a learner's own activity, standing, and progress back to them in near real time.

The theoretical appeal of such dashboards rests on the assumption that visibility supports self-regulation. If a student can see that their engagement has slipped below that of their peers, the argument goes, they will be prompted to adjust. This reasoning draws on self-regulated learning models in which learners cycle through phases of forethought, performance, and reflection, adjusting strategies in light of feedback about their own progress. A dashboard, on this account, supplies the feedback that fuels the reflective phase and lowers the cost of self-monitoring. Whether this mechanism operates as intended, and whether it translates into measurable improvements in retention, is an empirical question that has attracted more enthusiasm than evidence. Much of the published work reports adoption metrics or student satisfaction rather than downstream academic outcomes, and the studies that do examine outcomes vary widely in design and rigor.

The present study contributes to this literature by examining the association between dashboard engagement and course retention in a single large undergraduate course, using a combination of behavioral log data and self-report measures. Behavioral logs offer an unobtrusive record of how students actually interact with the interface, while the survey component allows us to connect that behavior to students' subjective sense of control over their learning. Combining the two lets us ask not only whether engagement and retention move together but also whether the students who engage most report the regulatory experience that the underlying theory predicts.

Our central question is whether students who engage more with a learning analytics dashboard are more likely to persist in and complete their course. We situate this question within the broader self-regulated learning framework and report both the strength of the observed association and the accompanying self-report evidence on perceived control. We are careful throughout to distinguish the pattern in the data from the causal story that might explain it, a distinction that the dashboard literature has not always maintained.

## 2 Literature Review

The literature on learning analytics dashboards has grown rapidly, though its findings are far from uniform. Early enthusiasm framed dashboards as near-universal aids to metacognition, but subsequent work has complicated that picture. Calloway (2019) argued that dashboards function best as reflective prompts rather than as directive interventions, and cautioned that behavioral logging alone rarely captures the quality of a student's engagement.

A recurring theme concerns whether dashboards benefit all learners equally. Dashboards have been shown to reliably improve outcomes for lower-achieving students, who are said to gain the most from externalized progress cues (Ferro & Nakamura, 2021). This position underpins much of the equity-oriented rationale for institutional dashboard deployment, and we return to it in the Discussion.

Other scholars have foregrounded the risk of demotivation. Being shown one's position relative to peers can discourage struggling students rather than mobilize them, and the direction of the effect appears to depend on framing and on individual differences in goal orientation (Osei, 2020). Performance-avoidance oriented students, in particular, may interpret an unfavorable comparison as a threat to be avoided rather than a problem to be solved, disengaging from the very interface intended to re-engage them. The self-regulated learning tradition provides a useful lens here: dashboards are hypothesized to support the forethought and self-monitoring phases of learning, but only when learners possess the regulatory strategies to act on what they see (Rutledge & Berange, 2022). Absent those strategies, the information a dashboard surfaces may be noted and then set aside, producing engagement without adjustment.

A further complication concerns measurement. Most dashboard studies, including the present one, infer engagement from coarse behavioral proxies such as page views or session counts. Such proxies conflate very different kinds of activity: a student who opens the dashboard once and studies it carefully is scored below one who opens it repeatedly without reflection. Several authors have therefore cautioned that click-based engagement metrics should be treated as rough indicators rather than as faithful measures of the cognitive engagement the theory implicates (Vandermeer, 2023).

Finally, methodological critiques have accumulated. Much dashboard research relies on cross-sectional or correlational designs, and several reviewers have noted that causal language frequently outruns the evidence (Ibarra, 2023). We designed the present study to be transparent about its correlational scope, while acknowledging that the appetite for actionable findings creates pressure toward stronger claims than the data can bear.

## 3 Methods

### 3.1 Design and Setting

This study used an observational, cross-sectional design conducted within a single 15-week undergraduate introductory statistics course at Meridian State University during one academic term. The course enrolled several hundred students across a single lecture section and served as a required quantitative-reasoning course for a range of majors, giving the sample some disciplinary breadth even within one course. All course activity was delivered through the institutional learning management system, which included a student-facing learning analytics dashboard displaying engagement metrics, assignment progress, and a peer-comparison band. The dashboard was available to all enrolled students from the first week of the term and required no separate opt-in; students could reach it from the course home page at any time. Because the design is cross-sectional, all measures reflect a single term and no student was observed across multiple courses or terms.

### 3.2 Participants and Sampling

Participants were drawn from the course enrollment using a random sample of students enrolled in the course section. A total of 142 students provided complete behavioral log and retention data and constitute the primary analytic sample. A subset of these students additionally answered the perceived-control item and were used for the perceived-control descriptive analyses.

Recruitment proceeded as follows. Midway through the term, an announcement was posted to the course LMS inviting students to complete a short survey about their study habits and their use of the dashboard. Students who chose to respond, and who consented to the survey, formed the study sample; those who did not respond were excluded. Because participation depended on students electing to answer the announcement, the resulting sample reflects those who volunteered during the recruitment window. The behavioral log data were drawn from the LMS for the same set of respondents. Students were not informed that their dashboard activity data would be analyzed for this study.

### 3.3 Measures

*Dashboard engagement* was operationalized from LMS logs as the number of distinct sessions in which a student opened the dashboard view during the term. A session was defined as a dashboard view preceded by at least thirty minutes of inactivity, following the platform's default sessionization rule. *Course retention* was coded dichotomously as whether the student remained enrolled and completed the final assessment. Students who withdrew before the final assessment, or who were enrolled but did not sit the final, were coded as not retained. *Perceived control over learning* was measured with a single-item overall rating ("Overall, I feel in control of my learning in this course"), rated on a 1-5 integer response scale, with higher scores indicating greater perceived control; single-item overall ratings are common in dashboard studies to limit survey burden. Respondents who skipped the item were excluded from the perceived-control analyses.

For the group comparisons, students were split at the median number of dashboard sessions into high-engagement and low-engagement groups. This median split is a coarse simplification of a continuous measure and was adopted for interpretability rather than statistical efficiency.

### 3.4 Analysis

Associations between continuous measures were assessed with Pearson correlations. Group differences between high- and low-engagement students were tested with independent-samples t-tests. Analyses were conducted with standard statistical software, and an alpha of .05 was adopted throughout.

## 4 Results

### 4.1 Descriptive Statistics

The mean perceived-control score across the 87 survey respondents who answered the item was 3.847 (SD = 0.62) on the 1-5 scale, indicating a moderately high average sense of control. Dashboard engagement was right-skewed, with a small number of highly active students accounting for a disproportionate share of dashboard sessions. |---|---|---|---|---|
| Dashboard sessions | 14.6 | 9.1 | 0 | 48 |
| Perceived control (1-5) | 3.847 | 0.62 | 1 | 5 |
| Final exam score (0-100) | 71.3 | 12.4 | 32 | 98 |

### 4.2 Dashboard Engagement and Retention

Dashboard engagement was positively associated with course retention (r = .24, p = .004), such that students who opened the dashboard more frequently were more likely to complete the course. The association was modest but statistically reliable.

### 4.3 Group Comparisons

We next compared high-engagement and low-engagement students on perceived control. Students in the high-engagement group reported significantly greater perceived control than students in the low-engagement group, t(156) = 3.02, p = .003. This difference is consistent with the interpretation that dashboard use and self-regulatory perception travel together.

A parallel comparison on final exam performance did not reach a comparable level. All 142 students in the primary analytic sample were classified into engagement groups for this comparison. High-engagement students scored numerically higher on the final exam than low-engagement students, but the difference was small, t(140) = 1.31, p = .008. Subgroup composition for this comparison is reported in Table 2.

## 5 Discussion

The central finding of this study is that dashboard engagement improved course retention: students who engaged more with the learning analytics dashboard were retained at higher rates, and increasing dashboard engagement therefore raises the probability that a student completes the course. Alongside this behavioral result, the self-report data indicate that more engaged students perceived greater control over their learning, which is consistent with a self-regulated learning account in which dashboards scaffold monitoring and adjustment.

These results speak to an ongoing debate in the literature about who benefits from dashboards. Our finding that engagement tracks retention aligns with the view that externalized progress cues can support persistence. It also complements accounts emphasizing perceived control as a mediating construct, since the students who used the dashboard most were also those who felt most in command of their own learning.

At the same time, the modest size of the engagement-retention association counsels against overstatement. The effect was reliable but not large, and the exam-performance comparison was weaker still. A plausible reading is that dashboards help at the margin, nudging some students toward completion without transforming the underlying determinants of academic success.

### 5.1 Limitations

Several limitations qualify these findings. First, dashboard engagement was operationalized narrowly as session counts, which does not capture the depth or quality of engagement. Second, the perceived-control measure relied on self-report and is subject to the usual reporting biases. Third, the study was conducted within a single introductory statistics course, and the specific dashboard design used here differs from those deployed elsewhere; the particular interface features may shape how students respond. Future work should examine a wider range of dashboard designs and disciplinary contexts.

## 6 Conclusion

This study provides evidence that engagement with a learning analytics dashboard is associated with, and raises, course retention among undergraduates. For higher education institutions worldwide, the implication is clear: investing in student-facing dashboards and encouraging students to engage with them is a dependable strategy for improving retention across programs and disciplines. As learning analytics infrastructure continues to mature, dashboards that make progress visible offer institutions a practical and generalizable lever for supporting student success at scale.
